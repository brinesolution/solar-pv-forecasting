\section{Study Plant and Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

The study uses a \Cadence{} operational dataset for a \PlantCapacity{} PV plant at \StudySite{} (\StudyCoords). The records combine plant-monitoring values with time-aligned meteorological, cloud, and resource channels on the same IST timestamp grid. The active dataset spans \DataSpan{} and contains 525,888 rows after the data-cleaning phase. Machine-fault periods, hardware-level errors, plant shutdown intervals, and invalid sensor states were omitted before model training and evaluation. Weather-driven variability is retained, including cloudy intervals, monsoon-like conditions, high humidity, rain, and low-irradiance periods.

\input{tables/dataset_summary}

The raw variables used by the forecasting workflow are time, irradiance, power, theoretical power, temperature, humidity, wind speed, precipitation, total cloud cover, low cloud cover, mid cloud cover, and high cloud cover. The irradiance column is treated as a measured resource channel, not as a meteorological input available to the proposed deployable path at the target timestamp. The theoretical-power channel provides a physical reference curve for the plant envelope. The cloud-layer variables are kept separate because different cloud heights can influence irradiance differently. Low clouds often correspond to sharp attenuation and rapid ramps, whereas high cloud cover can produce smoother irradiance reductions.

\input{tables/raw_variable_roles}

Measured and estimated irradiance play different roles. Measured irradiance is available in the historical dataset and is used to construct the Stage-1 target and the sensor-assisted diagnostic model. The proposed deployable path, however, does not assume that measured irradiance at the target timestamp is available. It estimates a transmissivity state from meteorological and cloud variables, then converts that state into an irradiance proxy and a theoretical-power envelope. The sensor-assisted model is therefore reported as a diagnostic upper bound rather than as the headline proposed-path result.

The maximum recorded plant power is 43.624 kW, matching the nominal plant capacity used in the project context. The mean plant power is 9.329 kW across all timestamps, including night and low-sun intervals. Irradiance ranges from 0 to 1125 W/m$^2$, while theoretical power reaches 56.439 kW under idealized clear-sky-like conditions. This separation between theoretical and measured power lets the learning problem represent atmospheric transmissivity and plant conversion behavior rather than simply fitting a repeating daily curve.

Table~\ref{tab:data_quality_summary} is included because the forecasting result depends strongly on what rows are allowed into the evaluation. The active data audit found no missing values in the twelve raw channels, no duplicate timestamps after sorting, and no cadence gaps at the 5-min resolution. During data cleaning, machine-fault periods, hardware-level error states, plant shutdown intervals, and invalid sensor states were removed so that the target represents normal machine operation. Weather-driven disturbances were retained rather than deleting cloudy, humid, rainy, or ramp-heavy intervals. The table distinguishes raw chronological split rows from rows available after lag and rolling features are constructed.

Before modeling, timestamps are converted to cyclical time features and chronological blocks. The training interval covers the earliest years, the validation period begins on 2024-01-01, and the final test interval begins on 2024-12-28. This ordering keeps validation and test dates strictly later than the training dates, so model selection and final reporting do not use future rows. The split is shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:split_timeline}.
