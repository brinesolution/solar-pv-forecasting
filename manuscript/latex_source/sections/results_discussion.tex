\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Headline Test Performance}

\input{tables/metrics_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:headline_metrics} reports the post-processed daylight performance of the proposed target-weather-input path. The evaluation gives an RMSE of \RMSEproposed, an nRMSE of \NRMSEproposed, an MAE of \MAEproposed, and an $R^2$ of \RtwoProposed{} on \Ntest{} daylight rows. The values are computed from the stated feature definitions, model outputs, Stage-1 clipping, and post-processing rule, so the reported test metrics are tied to a reproducible offline protocol rather than to a lead-time-specific weather-forecast archive.

The positive bias of 0.2857 kW indicates a mild tendency to over-predict during daylight intervals. The error is still small relative to the 43.624 kW plant rating, but it matters for energy scheduling because a persistent positive bias can accumulate over many 5-min intervals. The sensor-assisted diagnostic path, which uses measured irradiance-derived inputs at the target timestamp, reaches 0.5228 kW RMSE. This gap shows that much of the remaining error comes from estimating the solar resource from meteorological and cloud variables rather than from the final XGBoost power-conversion step alone.

The figure helps show whether the reported RMSE comes from many small deviations or from a smaller number of hard ramp events.

\subsection{Baseline Skill and Forecast Interpretation}

\input{tables/baseline_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:baseline_summary} separates the two persistence comparisons and prevents a single ambiguous skill claim. Against previous-day same-time persistence, the proposed model reduces RMSE from 5.4613 kW to 2.8277 kW on the common daylight mask, corresponding to a skill score of 0.4822. Against 5-min last-value persistence, however, the proposed model has negative skill because last-value persistence uses live plant power immediately before the target timestamp and therefore behaves as a strong nowcast. The two baselines should not be collapsed into one claim.

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/results/baseline_rmse_skill_comparison.png}
\caption{RMSE comparison against sensor-assisted and persistence references. Previous-day persistence and 5-min last-value persistence represent different information settings.}
\label{fig:baseline_skill}
\end{figure}

The model is therefore positioned as a target-weather-input predictor, not as a replacement for every short-horizon nowcast. It is relevant when weather inputs are available for target timestamps and measured target-time irradiance or live plant power is not part of the feature set. It is not presented as a replacement for a live last-value nowcast when the horizon is only one 5-min step and live SCADA power is available.

\input{tables/error_taxonomy}

Table~\ref{tab:error_taxonomy} translates the diagnostic figures into engineering error modes. This view adds information that a single aggregate RMSE cannot provide. For example, a model can be strong under previous-day persistence comparison and still be inadequate for ramp-aware battery dispatch if the largest errors cluster around fast cloud-edge events. Conversely, a model that loses to 5-min last-value persistence can still be relevant for schedule-oriented or forecast-input workflows where live target-adjacent plant power is not available.

\subsection{Ablation Results}

\input{tables/ablation_summary}

Table~\ref{tab:ablation_summary} gives matched-pipeline ablations. The two-stage cascade improves over a direct single-stage XGBoost model by reducing daylight RMSE from 3.0382 kW to 2.8251 kW. Removing low-, mid-, and high-cloud channels while keeping total cloud cover gives 2.8490 kW RMSE, so the cloud-layer gain is smaller but still measurable. The ablation pattern indicates that the main contribution is an operationally reproducible physics-guided pipeline, not a large algorithmic breakthrough.

\subsection{Weather-Regime Behavior}

Weather-regime analysis exposes where the model is reliable and where it should be treated carefully. In the held-out test results, daylight MAE is 1.275 kW for clear intervals, 1.742 kW for partly cloudy intervals, and 2.237 kW for overcast intervals. The corresponding RMSE values are 1.730 kW, 2.411 kW, and 3.189 kW. Overcast periods dominate the difficult cases because a single total cloud-cover value, even with cloud-layer separation, cannot fully describe local cloud thickness, advection, and irradiance intermittency at a 5-min cadence.

\subsection{Interpretation and Claim Boundaries}

The results support a focused claim. The model is not universally superior to persistence, because the live 5-min last-value baseline is stronger under the present test setup. The supported claim is that the proposed target-weather-input pipeline improves over previous-day same-time persistence and over matched structural ablations while remaining physically interpretable. Deployment from forecast-style weather inputs is a compatible use case, but it requires target-time weather fields to be supplied before issue time and should be evaluated separately for each forecast lead time.

The sensor-assisted model has a narrower role. It is not the main reported path because it uses measured irradiance-derived features at the target timestamp. It still estimates an upper bound on the plant-data mapping when the resource signal is known. The difference between 0.5228 kW RMSE for the sensor path and 2.8251 kW RMSE for the proposed path indicates that future improvements should focus on resource estimation: better weather forecasts, sky imagery, clear-sky-index persistence, local irradiance sensing, or probabilistic cloud-motion features.

The ablation results provide a second claim boundary. The two-stage structure gives a meaningful improvement over direct single-stage XGBoost, but the cloud-layer improvement over total cloud cover alone is modest. Low-, mid-, and high-cloud features add measurable information in this dataset and help preserve interpretability, while the larger modeling decision is the two-stage transmissivity-to-power decomposition.

Finally, the physical post-processing should be treated as a transparent engineering rule, not as proof that XGBoost is intrinsically physically constrained. The final prediction is bounded because Eqs.~\eqref{eq:kt_clip}--\eqref{eq:postprocess} clip the Stage-1 transmissivity estimate, construct a nonnegative theoretical-power envelope, and enforce the plant rating. This prevents impossible reported outputs, but it is not a monotonic constraint, constrained loss, or proof of physical consistency in every internal tree split.
